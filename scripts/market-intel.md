# Intelligence Dossier: St Andrews Student Lettings Market

**St Andrews faces the most structurally constrained student housing market of any major UK university town.** With roughly **10,500–11,600 students** occupying a coastal town of just ~20,000 residents, the student-to-population ratio approaches 58% — more than double that of Oxford or Cambridge. The university houses approximately **4,100 students** directly, leaving 6,000–7,500 dependent on a private rental market capped by a controversial HMO overprovision policy, squeezed by short-term holiday lets, and characterised by rents that consume up to **88% of the maximum Scottish maintenance loan**. The crisis peaked in summer 2022, when up to 400 students reported being without accommodation weeks before term, some sleeping in cars, with the university forced to secure emergency beds **14 miles away in Dundee**. While new developments totalling ~1,850 beds are in the pipeline for 2024–2026, the fundamental mismatch between a threefold expansion in student numbers since 2002 and a tiny, geographically isolated town with negligible housing growth capacity remains the defining structural challenge.

---

## 1. How the market is structured

The St Andrews lettings market operates across four distinct segments: university-managed accommodation, private HMO lets, purpose-built student accommodation (PBSA), and an increasingly dominant short-term let sector that directly competes for the same housing stock.

**University accommodation** provides approximately **4,100 bed spaces** across 12–14 named halls and residences, housing roughly **40% of the student body** — an unusually high proportion by UK standards. Key residences include St Salvator's Hall, Agnes Blackadder Hall, David Russell Apartments, Fife Park, and Andrew Melville Hall, offering a mix of catered and self-catered options ranging from **£6,246/year** (self-catered standard at Gannochy House, 34 weeks) to **£12,976/year** (catered en-suite at Whitehorn Hall, 38 weeks). The university's accommodation guarantee covers all first-year undergraduate entrants, care-experienced students for the duration of their course, and international postgraduates on a limited basis. Returning undergraduates and home-fee postgraduates receive **no guarantee** and must compete in a February ballot or enter the private market.

**The private HMO sector** represents roughly **974 licensed properties** in St Andrews — an extraordinary **85% of all HMOs in Fife** — with an HMO density of approximately **14.9%** of housing stock. This is nearly seven times the concentration in Dundee or Edinburgh (1.1–2.3%). Seven letting agents are officially listed by the university: **Lawson & Thompson** (specialist student agent managing ~75 properties, winner of Sunday Times Letting Agent of the Year 2015), **DJ Alexander** (Scotland-wide firm with a St Andrews branch), **Bradburne & Co**, **Delmor**, **Inchdairnie Properties**, **Rollos**, and **Alba**. Additional agents include Thorntons Lettings, STAND Property, St Andrews Property Lets, and Eve Brown Property Services. The university also operates **Studentpad** (standrewsstudentpad.co.uk) as its official private accommodation search platform.

**Purpose-built student accommodation (PBSA)** is a small but growing segment. **SPACE St Andrews** opened for 2024/25 with **208 rooms** (studios and shared apartments), winning Scottish Property Awards Student Accommodation of the Year 2026. **Ayton House** (Hello Student) and **East Shore** (Homes for Students) also operate in the town. The major pipeline project is **Albany Park**, a £100 million partnership between the university and Campus Living Villages delivering **703 beds** across six buildings, with earliest occupation targeted for **autumn 2026**.

**Short-term lets** have become a significant competitor. Approximately **1,000 licensed short-term lets** operate in St Andrews, representing **5.4% of the town's housing stock**. Combined with HMOs, these two categories account for roughly **20% of all St Andrews housing**. Landlords increasingly prefer short-term lets as more profitable — one student reported a 47% rent rise when their flat was converted to a "luxury retreat" marketed at £305/night during the summer golf season.

### Student population figures

The University of St Andrews reports **10,356 students** for 2024/25 on its official facts page, though other sources cite figures up to 11,645. Approximately **79% are undergraduates** (~9,180) and **21% postgraduates** (~2,460). Around **45% are international students** representing over 120 nationalities, with roughly 20% from North America. The annual undergraduate intake is estimated at **2,200–2,500** based on UCAS data showing ~20,000 applications and a ~24.7% offer rate. Student numbers have grown dramatically — from roughly 3,000 in 2002 to over 10,000 by 2020, a level originally not planned until 2024.

## 2. A market defined by scarcity and speed

The supply-demand imbalance in St Andrews is not merely tight — it is **structurally dysfunctional**. The gap between the ~10,500 student population and ~4,100 university beds means at least **6,000–6,500 students** must find private accommodation in a town with fewer than 7,000 total dwellings, of which a significant share is occupied by permanent residents, used as holiday lets, or otherwise unavailable.

The crisis reached its acute peak in **August 2022**. The university acknowledged **130 students** had directly contacted its accommodation services unable to find housing. The Campaign for Affordable Student Housing (CASH), a student advocacy group, conducted a survey reporting **350–400 students without accommodation** just two to three weeks before term began — a figure the university disputed as "without foundation." CASH reported that **40% of these homeless students were entering their second year**, the critical transition point from guaranteed halls to the private market. Students reportedly slept in cars and sofa-surfed.

The university's emergency response was to secure beds at **The Old Mill, Dundee** (operated by Mears Student Life) at **£118/week**, roughly 14 miles and nearly one hour's commute from campus. Approximately **140 students** were offered this placement. A subsequent CASH survey found **86% of students placed in Dundee reported negative mental health impacts**, 96% of first-years said the separation had "a serious negative impact" on their studies, and 81% viewed Dundee as a last resort.

Several structural factors compound the shortage. First, student numbers grew unsustainably — the university admitted **20 times more postgraduate students in 2020 than in 2019**, coinciding with pandemic-era grade inflation that inflated undergraduate intake. Second, the **HMO cap** (detailed in Section 4) has reduced available beds rather than simply freezing them. Third, the **demolition of Albany Park** in 2018/2020 — previously the most affordable hall at roughly **£3,500/year** — removed 340 beds with no replacement until 2026 at earliest. Fourth, the conversion of long-term rental stock to **Airbnb and holiday lets**, accelerated by the 2022 Open Championship at St Andrews.

### The scramble culture

Properties in St Andrews let at extraordinary speed. The letting cycle operates on a compressed and stressful timeline: students arriving in **October must form flatmate groups immediately** and begin preparations, because letting agent property lists are released in **late January or early February** and are effectively first-come-first-served. One university alumni blog described the process as a "military campaign": students gather before 9am on release day, divide up viewing responsibilities, race between properties, and rush back to the agent's office to submit applications — with no notification system to indicate when a property has already been taken.

The Students' Association's 2025 How to Rent guide acknowledges the reality directly: "Finding a home in St Andrews shouldn't be a battle. But for too many students, renting has become one of the biggest sources of stress." Students report applying to **15 or more properties**, joining "sketchy" Facebook groups, and approaching virtual strangers about flat plans. University-managed houses are allocated by **random ballot** and described as "purely down to luck." Over **1,500 students** now commute to St Andrews from Leuchars, Dundee, Cupar, and wider Fife — a population that has grown significantly due to "accommodation shortage, financial difficulties, and pressures from the cost of living crisis."

By 2023–24, the situation appeared to partially self-correct, with the university reporting **"several hundred vacancies"** in its own residences — likely because the 2022 panic drove students to secure private housing earlier and more aggressively. This cyclical pattern does not, however, resolve the underlying structural shortage.

## 3. Rents that outstrip every Scottish city

St Andrews is the **most expensive student rental market in Scotland** and among the most expensive in the UK outside London. According to a DJ Alexander market overview from April 2023, the **average monthly rent across all property types in St Andrews was £1,620** — **60% higher than Edinburgh** (£1,014), more than double Glasgow (£780), and more than double Aberdeen (£706). While this figure includes non-student luxury and tourist properties, student-specific rents remain exceptionally high.

**Private rents per student per month** in shared accommodation, based on CASH's crowdsourced rent map (2024), range from **£400–£600** on the outskirts (Tom Morris Drive) to **£700** in mid-range areas (Lamond Drive) to **£900+** in prime locations (North Street, Queen's Gardens). Studentpad listings in 2024 showed properties ranging from a 2-bed former council house at **£1,500/month** to a 5-bed at **£3,925/month**. Studios and one-bedroom flats typically command **£600–£1,000+ per month**.

A 2022 Arora Medical Education study ranked St Andrews as the **most expensive Scottish university town** for total weekly student living costs at **£188.50** (of which rent comprised £152/week), compared to Edinburgh at £183.70, Glasgow at £155.30, and Dundee at £141. In 2018, St Andrews was already the **third most expensive UK university town** for student rent at ~£543/month, close to London's £584.

### Rent inflation has been steep

Rents have risen sharply across multiple measures. The average student rent rose from approximately **£527/month in 2019** (RBS Student Living Index) to **£760/month in 2022** (Arora study) — a **44% increase** in three years. Individual students have reported even steeper rises: one told The Courier their rent increased from **£816 to £1,200/month**, a 47% jump in a single year coinciding with their flat's conversion to a "luxury retreat." University hall fees saw an **8.3% average increase** announced for 2023–24, with a proposed 7.5% increase for 2025–26 subsequently negotiated down to **2.7%** after Students' Association lobbying.

NUS Scotland research found that PBSA rents nationally increased **34% between 2018 and 2023**, with Scotland's student rents rising faster than any other UK region during 2018–2021.

### Affordability against student income

The affordability picture is stark. Scottish students from households earning under £20,999 receive a maximum total support package of approximately **£11,400/year** (comprising maintenance loan, special support loan from 2024/25, and bursary). NUS Scotland found that average student rent in Scotland consumed **88% of the maximum maintenance loan**, leaving just **£22.42/week** for food, transport, books, and all other expenses. At St Andrews specifically, even the cheapest university hall (Gannochy House at £6,246/year) absorbs **55–65% of total student support**, while private rents of £600–£900/month over nine months (£5,400–£8,100) consume **47–85%**. The most expensive catered halls at £12,976/year **exceed the maximum support package entirely**.

NUS UK's 2024 housing survey found **34% of Scottish students** struggled to pay rent in full — higher than the UK national average of 26%. Among Scottish respondents, 19% had used a food bank and 45% had gone without heating.

## 4. The HMO cap that became a flashpoint

Fife Council's HMO overprovision policy is the single most contested regulatory feature of the St Andrews housing market. Under the **Housing (Scotland) Act 2006** and the **Private Rented Housing (Scotland) Act 2011**, local authorities gained discretionary power to refuse HMO licences on overprovision grounds. In Scotland, any property occupied by **three or more unrelated persons** sharing basic amenities requires an HMO licence.

**The policy timeline** begins with a 2011 temporary planning moratorium on new HMOs in the St Andrews Central Conservation Area, which was extended repeatedly. An independent 2017 review by North Star Consulting found the moratorium had "failed to do as intended," pushing HMO provision into surrounding residential areas. Following a public consultation in 2018–19 in which **78% of households favoured no growth** while **90% of students favoured allowing growth** (though only 9% of students responded), Fife Council's Community & Housing Services Committee **unanimously adopted a "zero growth" overprovision policy on 11 April 2019**, covering the entire St Andrews area.

The consequences have been counterproductive in several respects. Since the cap was imposed, St Andrews has **lost 17 HMOs — a total of 124 beds**. More significantly, the university estimates that **200+ bedrooms sit unoccupied** in properties where landlords restrict occupancy to two students to avoid triggering the three-person HMO threshold — the so-called "locked bedroom" phenomenon. Following a three-year review and public consultation closing in January 2023, the Fife Cabinet Committee reached a **compromise in 2023**: maintaining the overall cap while permitting **15 university-managed HMO licences over three years**. A further consultation has recently closed (2025), with results pending publication.

**HMO licensing requirements** in Fife include landlord registration, gas safety certificates, electrical condition reports, fire risk assessments, public liability insurance of at least £5 million, and floor plans. Licences last three years. Fees operate on a sliding scale from **£1,480 for 3–5 occupants** upward, replacing a previous flat fee of £1,300 for new applications. Operating without a licence carries a maximum penalty of **£50,000**.

## 5. What the university has done and failed to do

The University of St Andrews provides **more accommodation per head than almost any other UK university outside Oxbridge colleges** — roughly 4,100 beds for ~10,500 students. It has committed to a target of **5,000 university-owned beds** through a capital programme exceeding **£100 million**.

The flagship project is **Albany Park**, where the original 1970s residence (340 beds, the cheapest at ~£3,500/year) was demolished in 2018/2020. A redesigned 703-bed, six-building development in partnership with Campus Living Villages received unanimous planning approval from Fife Council in November 2023, with over 30% standard-priced rooms. Construction was expected from spring 2024, with earliest occupation in **autumn 2026**. An additional **148 beds** at Gap Site 3 (North Haugh) have also secured planning consent.

The university's emergency response to the 2022 crisis included securing beds in Dundee, establishing a **direct leasing scheme** (managing private landlord properties and acting as guarantor), lobbying Fife Council for HMO cap reform, and offering bus pass reimbursement for displaced students. It has also provided commuter support infrastructure including food preparation spaces and locker storage across campus.

However, the university faces criticism from multiple directions. Residents' groups, particularly the **Confederation of St Andrews Residents' Associations (CSARA)**, accuse it of growing too fast — from ~3,000 students in 2002 to over 10,000 by 2020 — while delaying replacement of demolished accommodation. Students and CASH criticise the university for disputing the scale of the crisis and for hall rent increases. The university's August 2022 statement acknowledged an "acute shortage of private-sector accommodation" while attributing causes to the HMO cap, Airbnb conversions, and pandemic-era grade inflation. It labelled CASH's homelessness figures as "persistently false claims."

## 6. The human cost of housing insecurity

The welfare impact on students is documented across multiple sources. NUS Scotland's landmark **"Broke Students, Broken System" report (2022)**, surveying 3,500+ Scottish students, found **12% had experienced homelessness** since starting their studies, rising to one in three among care-experienced and estranged students. **64% experienced mental ill-health** due to financial pressures, and 35% considered dropping out. The follow-up **"Cost of Survival" report (2024)**, surveying 5,300+ students, found **52% had skipped meals** due to lack of money, **13% had been unable to find housing** by the time classes began, and **37% considered dropping out** for financial reasons.

At St Andrews specifically, the 2022 crisis produced acute distress. The Scotsman reported parents "being forced to consider deferring or withdrawing" students, with one parent prepared to "fly to St Andrews and pay cash for a property." A final-year student told Bylines Scotland: "This experience was the number one thing that caused me stress this summer. There was a prolonged period in which I felt distressed and even considered taking a year away." The Saint documented the wealth dimension: students from affluent families can pay "extortionate prices" that systematically price out lower-income peers, creating what the paper called "The St Andrews Wealth Gap." A student who applied to over 15 properties described joining "sketchy Facebook groups" out of desperation.

**Rental fraud** has also targeted vulnerable students. Three St Andrews students lost a combined **~£12,000** in accommodation scams, with Police Scotland confirming reports of fraudsters pretending to own properties and collecting deposits before disappearing. The Students' Association explicitly warns students never to pay deposits without viewing.

The Students' Association operates an Accommodation Subcommittee, provides a lease-checking service, partners with the **Marks Out Of Tenancy** platform for landlord reviews, and runs flatmate matchmaking sessions. CASH organised a **protest march in September 2022** with approximately 200 students marching through town, and co-director Barry Will used a megaphone outside Principal Sally Mapstone's home to accuse her of "directly responsible for manufacturing" the crisis.

## 7. Planning battles and the politics of town versus gown

Town-gown relations in St Andrews have been described as at an **"all-time low"** over housing, with the conflict playing out through Fife Council planning decisions, community campaigns, and local media.

**Residents' concerns** centre on what CSARA calls the "studentification" of St Andrews. Permanent residents now make up **less than half** the total population. In the Central Conservation Area, students comprise **80%+ of residents**. The birth rate in St Andrews was just **3% versus the Scottish average of 9%** in 2019/20, and Langlands Primary School closed in 2006 partly due to family displacement. Over **150 former council houses** have been purchased by buy-to-let landlords for HMO use, and **370–600+ people** sit on the council's social housing waiting list. Conservative councillor Linda Holt described the displacement of permanent residents as "a kind of social cleansing."

**Planning decisions** reflect these tensions. The **Kilrymont development** (Scotsman Developments) at the former Madras College site represents the largest private PBSA project: Phase 1 delivered 208 rooms in September 2024, Phase 2 (241 rooms) is due 2026, and Phase 3 (703 rooms) was approved in May 2025 by Fife Council's North East Fife Planning Committee by a vote of 6 to 4, over **34 objection letters** including from the St Andrews Community Council. SNP councillor Ann Verner said: "I'm really, really upset... It's going to kill that part of town." Liberal Democrat councillor Jane Ann Liston moved to refuse on overdevelopment grounds but was outvoted.

**FIFEplan** (the adopted local development plan, September 2017) specifically addresses HMOs in Policy 2, stating HMO use "will not be supported" for new dwellings unless purpose-built, or for conversions in areas with HMO restrictions. Student and holiday accommodation are explicitly excluded from qualifying as affordable housing. The emerging **Fife's Place Plan** is expected to update these policies.

Politically, all **four St Andrews ward councillors** supported removing the HMO cap, but none sat on the Cabinet Committee that made the decision to maintain it. North East Fife MSP Willie Rennie called for evidence gathering on the cap's impact. Dr Ross Brown of the University of St Andrews School of Management conducted the first independent assessment of studentification, finding **753 HMO licences granted since 2011** (435 in residential areas, 318 in the town centre, ~200 for university halls).

## 8. How the media has told this story

The St Andrews housing crisis has generated sustained coverage across Scottish and UK media, peaking in summer–autumn 2022.

**The Courier** (Dundee) has provided the most extensive ongoing coverage, including reports on scores of students struggling to find accommodation (August 2022), CASH-organised protests outside the Principal's home (September 2022), a two-year retrospective identifying St Andrews as "Scotland's most expensive seaside town" with average house prices of **£458,381** (2024), and a feature on the "death of community" as streets become "increasingly barren of children." **The Scotsman** published a major piece in August 2022 headlined "Prestigious Scottish university faces student housing crisis," featuring the now-iconic parental quote about being "willing to buy a property just to keep her in school." **STV News** provided television coverage of the "acute housing shortage" and the Dundee displacement.

**The Ferret**, Scotland's investigative journalism platform, published two significant pieces in 2018: one documenting town-gown relations at an "all-time low" with 600+ people on the housing waiting list, and another revealing through FOI requests that the university had 131 vacant rooms at term start even as it was blamed for the housing crisis. **Bylines Scotland** published an in-depth piece by CASH co-director Lily Von Geyer documenting the mental health toll, including the figure that 86% of Dundee-placed students reported negative mental health impacts.

**The Tab** included St Andrews in a national round-up of "every single term-one student housing disaster" in October 2022, alongside Glasgow, Exeter, Bristol, and Lincoln. An academic paper published in **Taylor & Francis** (2024) used St Andrews and Edinburgh as case studies for "studentification" and student housing precarity. The Saint, St Andrews' student newspaper, has published extensively on the crisis, wealth inequality in accommodation access, and planning developments.

Notably, **no dedicated long-form investigative piece from The Guardian or The Times** focused specifically on St Andrews housing was identified, though both outlets have covered the broader Scottish and UK student housing crises in which St Andrews features.

## 9. St Andrews in UK comparative context

St Andrews occupies a unique position among UK university towns with acute housing pressure, distinguished by three factors: the most extreme student-to-population ratio, the smallest physical footprint, and the greatest geographic isolation.

| Metric | St Andrews | Durham | Oxford | Cambridge |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Town/city population | ~20,000 | ~48,000 | ~150,000 | ~124,000 |
| Student population | ~10,500–11,600 | ~20,000–22,000 | ~36,000+ | ~24,000+ |
| Students as % of population | **~58%** | ~46% | ~24% | ~19% |
| Physical area | ~4.5 sq mi | ~8.4 sq mi | ~17.6 sq mi | ~16 sq mi |
| Nearest train station | Leuchars (5 mi) | Durham (central) | Oxford (central) | Cambridge (central) |
| College/halls guarantee | First year only | Most undergrads | Most undergrads | Most undergrads |

**Durham** is the closest comparator — a small city where students comprise roughly 45% of the population and studentification has transformed residential areas (Green Lane is 75% student housing). But Durham is three times larger, has a central railway station, and sits ten minutes from Newcastle's housing market. **Oxford and Cambridge** face high rents and town-gown friction but benefit from their collegiate systems, which guarantee housing for most undergraduates across multiple years, and their substantially larger populations absorb the student presence more easily. **Exeter** experienced its own crisis in 2022 (freshers housed in a Holiday Inn Express) but is a significantly larger city with more development capacity.

Scotland declared a **national housing emergency in May 2024**. A Cross-Party Group on Housing report identified massive shortfalls across Scotland's cities: **13,852 beds in Edinburgh, 6,093 in Glasgow, and 6,084 in Dundee** — totalling over 25,000 nationally. While St Andrews was not separately quantified in this report (it focuses on cities), its proportional shortage is arguably the most severe in the country.

## Conclusion

The St Andrews student lettings market is defined by a **structural contradiction**: a world-class university that has tripled in size occupying a medieval coastal town with essentially no capacity to grow. The HMO cap, intended to protect community balance, has inadvertently reduced available beds and inflated rents. Short-term lets have emerged as a more profitable alternative for landlords, further shrinking the long-term rental pool. New supply — roughly 1,850 beds across Albany Park, Gap Site 3, and Kilrymont — will begin arriving from 2024 through 2026, representing the **largest single expansion in a decade**. But even with these additions, the market will remain among the tightest and most expensive in the UK.

Three dynamics deserve particular attention for ongoing coverage. First, the **Housing (Scotland) Bill** at Stage 2 has voted to extend rent control powers to PBSA and university halls (CPI + 1%, max 6%) — if this survives Stage 3, it would fundamentally alter the economics of student accommodation in St Andrews. Second, the outcome of Fife Council's **latest HMO overprovision consultation** (2025, results pending) could either entrench or reform the cap. Third, the **cyclical nature of the crisis** — acute shortage in 2022 followed by surplus in 2023/24 — suggests that panic-driven early letting compounds the dysfunction, a pattern that any accommodation alerts platform could help mitigate through better information flow and market transparency.

The core insight for content strategy is that the St Andrews housing market is not merely expensive or competitive — it is **a case study in how regulatory, demographic, geographic, and economic forces can combine to create a uniquely dysfunctional market**, where the wealthiest students are insulated while lower-income students face genuine housing precarity. This is the narrative thread that connects supply data, rent figures, welfare evidence, and political context into a coherent and compelling story.
